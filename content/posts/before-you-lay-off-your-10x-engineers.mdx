A 10x engineer with AI doesn't become 11x.

They become 100x.

That's the math some companies are getting catastrophically wrong right now.

## The Multiplication Math

If AI turns a baseline engineer into a 10x contributor, it turns your senior engineers into 100x contributors. The ratio stays the same, but the absolute gap in output explodes.

Before AI, the difference between your best and average engineer was 9 units of work. After AI, it's 90.

And you're thinking about laying off the 100x people?

## What 10x Actually Means

The 10x engineer was never just fast at coding. They were fast at everything around the code.

They've been steeped in the industry long enough to know which trends matter and which ones are noise. They know the architectural decisions that seem fine today but cost you massive headaches in eighteen months. They navigate company politics well enough to actually get products shipped.

AI doesn't replace any of that. AI makes all of it more leveraged.

## I Watched It Happen

I watched a company cut their senior engineers last year, betting that Claude Code and Cursor would close the gap. Their remaining team shipped faster than ever. They also built an absolute mess.

The velocity was real. The progress wasn't.

Without senior engineers in the loop, nobody was asking the hard questions. Nobody was saying "we've seen this pattern before, and it doesn't scale." Nobody was pushing back on the architecture that looked clean in a PR but would collapse under production load.

## The Junior Dev With Superpowers Problem

One senior engineer put it perfectly: AI is like a junior dev who will dutifully set upon any task you give it, often taking unusual approaches when what you need is logical clarity, observability, efficiency, and security.

Another described watching a junior dev using Claude build a small web app. When it was done, every form submission fired twice. The AI insisted the user was pressing the button twice. After two hours of the AI chasing its own tail, the engineer spent two minutes troubleshooting and found the AI had created two event listeners for the same button.

AI is still terrible at troubleshooting the problems it creates. That's not a bug that gets patched. That's a fundamental gap that only experienced engineers can fill.

## The Real ROI Is Non-Linear

Here's the part most engineering leaders miss: the real AI ROI only works for engineers who fully embrace non-linear development. It's shocking how many engineers write a prompt and then sit there waiting for it to finish.

A traditional 10x engineer should be juggling five to ten AI agents at any given time. And it gets even better when those agents are delegating to more specialized agents. That's where the real magic happens—not in faster typing, but in orchestrating parallel streams of work that a less experienced engineer wouldn't even know how to decompose.

The senior engineer's superpower was never speed. It was knowing how to break a problem into the right pieces. AI just lets them break it into ten pieces simultaneously.

## Who Reviews the 100x Output?

One counterargument deserves attention: who code-reviews the output of a 100x engineer? There's a real tension between velocity and understanding.

Remember the old engineering triangle: good, fast, cheap—pick two. The concern is that AI shifts you toward fast and cheap with the illusion of good. Code that compiles and passes tests but lacks the guardrails only experience can provide.

This is exactly why you need the senior engineers in the loop. They're not just producing 100x output. They're the ones who can evaluate whether that output should exist at all. Remove them and you don't just lose a coder—you lose the only person who could tell you the code was wrong.

## The Institutional Knowledge You Can't Rebuild

There's something even more expensive than rehiring a 10x engineer: losing the years of institutional knowledge they carry. Claude can boost output, but it has zero knowledge of your product, your domain, or the decisions your team made three years ago and why.

Every senior engineer on your team is a walking decision log. They remember the migration that failed, the vendor that oversold, the architecture that almost sank a product launch. That context doesn't live in a wiki. It lives in their heads. And when they walk out the door, it walks out with them.

## They Are the Multiplier

The engineers who survived the last three evolutions of the web know when to say no. When to push back. They have an innate ability to get things done that goes far beyond writing code. They recruit teams, build culture, and lead to outcomes—not just output.

The smartest companies aren't treating their 10x engineers as individual contributors who happen to type faster. They're treating them as the future managers of AI agents—orchestrating teams of automated workers, passing on decades of wisdom to systems that can finally leverage it at scale. The engineers who see that will hit 100x. Maybe 1,000x.

Those are the people AI amplifies most. Removing them doesn't save money. It removes the multiplier.

## The Counterpoint Worth Hearing

Not everyone agrees with the 100x math, and it's worth being honest about that. Some argue that AI compresses work in log time—when tasks already take an hour, the gap between your best and average engineer narrows, not widens. Others point out that engineers who built their 10x reputation on pattern recognition and code generation—cranking out DAOs, DTOs, CRUD scaffolding—might actually go from 10x to 5x, because AI commoditized their exact superpower.

Fair points, both of them. But they prove my point: the engineers who matter most aren't the ones who were fast at the mechanical parts. They're the ones whose judgment, architecture instincts, and leadership can't be compressed by a logarithm.

## Do the Right Math

Before you cut headcount, do the math.

Not the salary math. The multiplication math.

And while you're at it—instead of doing the same work with fewer people, ask yourself why you aren't doing ten times the work with the same people. Serve ten customers instead of one. Ship ten products instead of one. That's the real leverage play.

Because the company that keeps their 100x engineers while competitors lay theirs off? That's the company that wins the next five years.
